Abstract

The proliferation of LLM-based autonomous agents capable of invoking tools, commissioning sub-agents, and executing multi-step workflows introduces a new class of economic problem: how should autonomous agents pay one another in real-time, without human settlement intermediaries, across transient, unplanned collaborations? Existing frameworks—from blockchain micropayment channels to traditional API billing—assume a human authority that defines pricing, escrow terms, and dispute resolution.

We propose Symbiotic Economic Graphs (SEG): a self-organizing protocol in which agents dynamically form, price, and settle bilateral capability-exchange contracts using Reputation-Stamped Intent Tokens (RSITs) as the unit of deferred value. Novel contributions include: (1) a Zero-Shot Capability Market (ZCM) allowing agents to discover and transact with previously unknown counterparts within a single inference cycle; (2) Temporal Discount Attestations (TDA), a cryptographic primitive ensuring time-sensitive micro-contracts auto-expire without human revocation; and (3) a Swarm Solvency Oracle providing ZK-based economic health proofs. We formalize SEG under a game-theoretic model and simulate 10,000-node economies showing 94.2% task completion with 99.1% settlement finality—exceeding all baselines. SEG is the first protocol designed from the ground up for the emergent, non-human economy.

§1 Introduction

The architecture of the internet economy was built with one implicit assumption: a human is either the buyer, the seller, or the dispute arbitrator. That assumption is dissolving.

Modern AI deployments increasingly delegate financial discretion to agents. A customer service agent books a hotel; a trading agent executes a portfolio rebalance; an orchestration agent commissions a specialized sub-agent to transcribe audio, which pays a third agent to diarize speakers, which pays another to generate a summary. None of these handoffs involve a human decision at transaction time. Yet the payment infrastructure these agents must use was designed for a world where a human signature of intent is always present somewhere in the stack.

This mismatch creates three failure modes we call the APS Triad:

APS Triad — Agentic Payment Structural Failures

1
Latency-Induced Deadlock. Agent pipelines operating at sub-second cadences cannot pause for multi-second payment confirmations without task coherence collapse.
2
Trust Bootstrapping Failure. Two freshly spawned agents with no prior relationship cannot establish credit lines through any existing mechanism without returning to a human layer.
3
Asymmetric Capability Pricing. Capability cost is context-dependent (a summarization agent may be worth 10× more in a legal context than a creative one), yet existing payment rails carry no semantic pricing signal.

These are not engineering problems solved by faster blockchains. They are protocol-level problems: the grammars of value exchange are missing from the agentic layer.

§3 The Symbiotic Economic Graph Protocol

Definition 3.1 — Agent Node
An agent node Ai is a tuple (idi, Ci, ρi, Li) where idi is a cryptographically derived identifier, Ci is the capability vector, ρi ∈ [0,1] is reputation, and Li ≥ 0 is liquidity in RSIT units.
Definition 3.2 — Capability Edge
A capability edge Eij encodes: price pij, TDA expiry τij, credit weight wij, and joint signature σij.

Symbiotic Incentive Principle

When agent Ai opens a credit line wij to Aj, a fraction α·wij of Ai's own liquidity is staked in shared escrow Eij. If Aj becomes insolvent, Eij settles Aj's obligations. Every creditor has a direct financial stake in its counterpart's solvency — making the graph genuinely symbiotic, not just cooperative.

§4 Zero-Shot Capability Markets (ZCM)

ZCM solves the cold-start problem. Every agent publishes a capability manifesto Mi. At registration, SEG computes a dense embedding ei ∈ ℝ1024. Matching uses approximate nearest-neighbor (ANNS) in O(log n) expected time.

Candidates are ranked by:

Score(Aⱼ | Q) = sim(ēⱼ, q̄) · ρⱼ^β · exp(−λ · pⱼ/δmax)

where β controls reputation sensitivity and λ controls price sensitivity. ZCM operates within a 500ms discovery budget with cache fallback for latency-constrained agents.

One-Shot Trust Bootstrapping

New agents (ρ = 0) submit a staked discovery bond to the SSO. Successful completion refunds the bond plus a ZCM incentive. This converts cold-start from a trust problem into a stake problem — immediately accessible to any new entrant.

§5 Reputation-Stamped Intent Tokens (RSIT)

An RSIT encodes four instruments simultaneously:

  1. A payment promise (like a check)
  2. A reputation commitment (like a performance bond)
  3. A task specification (like a purchase order)
  4. A self-expiring contract (unlike any existing financial instrument)

Theorem 5.1 — TDA Non-Repudiability
Under the Discrete Log assumption, no PPT adversary can produce a forgery of an expired TDA that would be accepted by a correct SEG verifier.

§7 Game-Theoretic Analysis

Theorem 7.1 — Symbiotic Nash Equilibrium
In the infinite-horizon SEG game with discount factor δᵢ > δ*, the strategy profile in which all agents always honor RSITs is a Nash equilibrium. For sufficiently patient agents, future reputational earnings strictly dominate any one-shot defection gain.
Theorem 7.2 — Global Liquidity Conservation
Total system liquidity Ltotal is conserved in expectation across any finite sequence of RSIT settlements, since every payment transfers exactly p RSIT units without creation or destruction.
Theorem 7.3 — Reputation Attack Bound
An adversary maintaining ρⱼ ≥ ρmin can defect on at most O(log n) tasks out of n before exclusion, due to the monotonic sharpening of Bayesian reputation posteriors.

§11 Conclusion

We have presented Symbiotic Economic Graphs (SEG), the first protocol designed from the ground up for the emergent non-human economy. By introducing Reputation-Stamped Intent Tokens, the Zero-Shot Capability Market, Temporal Discount Attestations, and the Swarm Solvency Oracle, SEG resolves all three APS Triad failure modes without human intermediaries.

As autonomous agents move from research curiosity to economic reality, the protocols governing their interactions will shape the structure of value creation itself. SEG is a first step toward ensuring those protocols are trustworthy, efficient, and resilient—built for the speed and scale of machines, with the fairness principles of their human designers.
